BCIs Are Here. Security Standards Aren't.

Brain-computer interfaces are being implanted in humans today — yet no universal security standard exists to protect the people who depend on them.

The Gap

Neuralink, BrainGate, and dozens of clinical trials are creating direct connections between silicon and neural tissue. Neuroethical principles like cognitive liberty, mental privacy, mental integrity, and psychological continuity remain largely theoretical — with no engineering framework to enforce them.

What's At Stake

ONI translates these principles into testable, implementable security controls — mapped across 14 layers, grounded in established physics, and aligned with UNESCO, FDA, and emerging neurorights legislation.

Why Now

The OSI model was built before networking was figured out. The periodic table left gaps for elements not yet discovered. ONI follows the same principle — organizing what we know, highlighting what we don't, and evolving as discovery fills in the gaps.

Research

An honest account of what ONI is, what it isn't, and where it's going

The power of mind is our frontier. We're making sure it's protected from day one with a universal, open standard that leaves no brain behind.

Core Mission

Brain-computer interfaces are here — and the field lacks a universal security standard. I am building that standard, grounded in neuroethics, informed by neuroscience, and enforced through engineering. My research sits at the intersection of neurosecurity, neuroethics, and neural engineering.

The Framework

ONI extends the OSI networking model with 7 additional layers for neural and cognitive systems. It provides a shared vocabulary for neurosecurity across disciplines, technical enforcement of neurorights (cognitive liberty, mental privacy, mental integrity, psychological continuity), and governance frameworks aligned with UNESCO, FDA, and emerging neurorights legislation. Read the whitepaper for the full technical overview.

What I've Built
  • 14-layer security model bridging networking and neuroscience
  • 10 governance documents — neuroethics alignment, UNESCO (15/17), informed consent, pediatric, regulatory compliance
  • Coherence Metric (Cₛ) — entropy-based signal validation with mathematical audit
  • TARA prototype — coherence scoring, rule-based neural firewall, defensive validation
  • 20+ publications across 8 research areas
Research Direction & Roadmap

Key questions I want to pursue:

  1. How do we translate neurorights into testable technical controls?
  2. What does informed consent look like for closed-loop BCIs that modify neural activity?
  3. How do we build security standards proactively — before exploits emerge?

A Note on Terminology

Foundational research — Kohno (2009), Bonaci (2015), and others — established the term "BCI security." ONI honors that terminology when referencing their work. Moving forward, ONI uses neurosecurity as the broader discipline name. "BCI security" is anchored to today's hardware. The threats ONI models (cognitive manipulation, identity fragmentation, semantic interference at L11–L14) aren't limited to computer interfaces. Neurosecurity protects the neural system itself, regardless of what interfaces with it.
